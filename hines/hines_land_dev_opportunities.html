1. Executive Summary

This report identifies and evaluates specific, actionable growth, partnership, certification, regulatory, platform, and competitive intelligence opportunities for Hines's land development business in Austin and Texas, following the April 2025 divestiture of Mirador and 10 other Texas master-planned communities to Starwood Capital for approximately $800M. The analysis focuses on opportunities appropriate for an institutional developer with $91.7B AUM, 151 active developments globally, and deep Austin market relationships via T3 Eastside, 333 Guadalupe, and the Domain pipeline.

Top 5 immediate priorities:

  1. Austin Transit Partnership TOD engagement — ATP's 15-station light rail system breaks ground 2027 with construction contracts already awarded; station-area development rights along the CapMetro/ATP corridor are the single most time-sensitive land opportunity in Austin for institutional developers.
  2. 333 Guadalupe density & regulatory optimization — Austin's HOME 2.0 ordinance, Affordability Unlocked density bonuses, and TIRZ 17 (North Burnet/Gateway) overlap create stacked incentive potential; Hines should retain Austin land-use counsel to model the interaction with the 1.9M SF GLO ground lease entitlements before the next City Council budget cycle (October 2026).
  3. Chapter 380 Economic Development Agreement pursuit — Austin has structured 380 agreements for major anchor developments; Hines's 333 Guadalupe project at this scale ($1B+ total investment) qualifies. No active Hines 380 agreement has been publicly disclosed for this site.
  4. Industrial land banking in Round Rock / Pflugerville / Hutto corridor — Austin absorbed 4.1M SF of industrial in 2025 against 3.2M SF of supply; land values in this corridor remain below $10/SF in some tracts despite strong BTS (build-to-suit) demand from semiconductor, logistics, and data center users.
  5. GIC / Oxford Properties / La Caisse (formerly Ivanhoé Cambridge) JV re-engagement — All three are actively deploying in US mixed-use and office in 2025–2026 with a contrarian stance on Sunbelt gateway markets; Hines's existing global relationships make these the likeliest near-term equity partners for 333 Guadalupe capitalization.

2. Growth Opportunities — Austin Land Development Pipeline

Austin's land development landscape is bifurcated: downtown and inner core face absorption headwinds, while the northern/eastern suburban corridors are absorbing industrial and BTR supply at pace. The highest-upside land opportunities for an institutional developer at Hines's scale are public-land RFPs, TOD sites, and data center / industrial land banking in the NE corridor.

2.1 — Public Land Dispositions & City RFPs

Austin Colony Park Sustainable Community — 208-Acre P3 Site

Priority: High Timeline: Immediate

Catellus is the master developer for Colony Park — a 208-acre city-owned site in Northeast Austin (off US-183 near Loyola Lane) planned as a mixed-income, mixed-use sustainable community. As Catellus's existing Austin master development relationship (Mueller, Colony Park) matures, individual vertical development parcels within Colony Park are beginning to come to market. Hines's mass-timber credentials from T3 Eastside and ESG positioning align exactly with the sustainability framework Austin requires for this site.

Scale: 208 acres total; individual parcels likely 5–30 acres; investment $50M–$300M per parcel depending on use.

Contact Catellus directly for Colony Park vertical development parcel availability: catellus.com/projects/colony-park/

Austin city contact: Austin Planning Department — Colony Park Small Area Planning lead, 512-974-7700

Austin Transit Partnership — Light Rail TOD Site Inventory (15 Stations)

Priority: High Timeline: 6-Month

ATP's light rail system (construction begins 2027, opens 2033) features 15 stations along a corridor that runs through downtown, East Austin, and South Congress. Stations with the highest development potential include: Republic Square (downtown core), East 6th Street (rapid gentrification corridor), Oltorf/South Congress (mixed-use density node), and Pleasant Valley (East Austin Opportunity Zone overlap). Station-area development rights along a 15-stop corridor with Lady Bird Lake bridge access represent the category of land acquisition that institutional developers win by relationship before sites are formally marketed.

Scale: Variable; station-area parcels in the 1–10 acre range, $30–$150/SF land value depending on location.

Contact: Austin Transit Partnership — atptx.org | 512-710-2100 | input@atptx.org

ATP selected Austin Rail Constructors (Stacy Witbeck + Sundt Construction JV) as its construction partner. ATP planning director is the correct contact for station-area development framework discussions.

Texas General Land Office (GLO) — Central Texas Ground Lease Pipeline

Priority: High Timeline: 6-Month

The GLO manages 13 million acres of Texas state land. The 333 Guadalupe ground lease is a GLO instrument; Hines's established GLO relationship is a competitive advantage for pursuing additional Central Texas GLO ground lease opportunities. The GLO's commercial real estate program includes surface leases, rights-of-way, and income-producing ground leases on state-owned urban land. Post-333 Guadalupe, the relationship should be actively maintained to monitor any additional GLO urban land coming to market in Austin, particularly near the Capitol Complex and UT Austin campus boundaries.

Scale: Ground leases are typically 50–99 year terms; negotiated on a case-by-case basis for state-owned land.

Contact: Texas GLO Real Estate Division — 1700 N. Congress Ave., Austin, TX 78701 | 1-800-998-4456 | glo.texas.gov/land/

Austin Housing Finance Corporation (AHFC) — Land Disposition RFPs

Priority: Medium Timeline: 6-Month

AHFC periodically issues RFPs for disposition of city-controlled land for mixed-income development. Austin Housing (512-974-3100) coordinates with AHFC on city-owned parcels designated for affordable housing components. At Hines's scale, the most relevant play is a mixed-income ground-floor residential component within a larger mixed-use structure — a structure that could attract AHFC land contributions at below-market basis in exchange for affordable unit commitments. AHFC's Affordability Unlocked bonus program (see Section 4) is the programmatic vehicle.

Scale: City land contribution at discounted basis; affordable unit requirement typically 10–20% of residential units at 60–80% AMI.

Contact: Austin Housing Department — 512-974-3100 | austintexas.gov/department/housing

Texas Facilities Commission — Surplus State Property

Priority: Medium Timeline: 12–18 Month

The Texas Facilities Commission manages surplus state-owned real property and periodically conducts competitive solicitations. The Capitol Complex area and surrounding neighborhoods in Central Austin include aging state office buildings with long-term redevelopment potential on TFC-controlled land. Given the Texas Legislature's direction toward consolidating state facilities, TFC dispositions in downtown Austin represent a category Hines should monitor. The Electronic State Business Daily (ESBD) at txsmartbuy.gov/esbd is the mandatory state procurement posting portal for all TFC solicitations.

Scale: Unknown until individual solicitations are issued; potentially 1–20 acres in urban core.

Contact: Texas Facilities Commission, Property Management Division — tfc.texas.gov | Austin, TX | Monitor ESBD: txsmartbuy.gov/esbd

2.3 — Opportunity Zone Investments

East Austin / Rundberg / North Lamar Opportunity Zone Tracts

Priority: High Timeline: Immediate — Dec 31, 2026 Deferral Deadline

Travis County contains multiple designated Qualified Opportunity Zones, concentrated in East Austin (along the ATP light rail corridor), North Lamar/Rundberg, and portions of Southeast Austin. The IRS's December 31, 2026 deferral deadline is the most critical OZ calendar milestone remaining — after this date, investors must recognize previously deferred gains. This creates a closing window where OZ fund vehicles seeking to deploy capital before the deadline will compete for Austin OZ land. Hines, as a potential co-investor or JV partner in an OZ-structured vehicle, can offer development expertise to a QOF sponsor. Key East Austin OZ census tracts overlap directly with the ATP light rail alignment — a dual TOD/OZ opportunity that is rare and high-value.

IRS deadline: Capital gains deferred into QOFs must be recognized December 31, 2026. Investors holding for 10+ years eliminate appreciation tax entirely. Fund vehicle minimums typically $5M–$25M equity.

IRS OZ resources: irs.gov/credits-deductions/opportunity-zones-frequently-asked-questions

Mapping: HUD OZ mapping tool — hud.gov/opportunity-zones

2.4 — New Growth Corridors

East Austin — Institutional Land Acquisition Activity

Priority: High Timeline: Immediate

East Austin (east of I-35, roughly Cesar Chavez to Airport Blvd) is the highest-velocity land acquisition corridor for institutional capital in 2025. The ATP light rail runs through it, two ATP Rapid Bus lines (800 and 837) launched in February 2025 with service increasing to 10-minute peak frequencies in Summer 2026, and multiple OZ tracts are present. Industrial-to-creative-office conversions, multifamily mixed-use, and life sciences adjacent to Dell Medical are all active uses. Competing institutional buyers include Lincoln Property, Endeavor Real Estate, and several REIT land-banking programs.

Land values: $40–$120/SF depending on proximity to transit and entitlements. Large assemblages (1+ acre) are increasingly rare and command premiums.

North Burnet / Gateway — Domain-Adjacent TIRZ 17 Submarket

Priority: High Timeline: Immediate

The North Burnet/Gateway regulating plan governs development along the Burnet Road corridor from US-183 to Koenig Lane, encompassing the Domain and adjacent areas. TIRZ 17 (North Burnet/Gateway) captures tax increment to fund public infrastructure that enables denser development. Hines's existing Domain office pipeline (608K SF) gives it credibility as a submarket anchor. Additional land parcels within TIRZ 17 that can benefit from public infrastructure funding represent the clearest near-term land acquisition opportunity adjacent to existing Hines activity. Brandywine's Uptown ATX (66-acre redevelopment at 11501 Burnet Road) is the primary competing large-scale institutional project in this submarket.

Land values: $30–$70/SF depending on entitlement status. TIRZ-funded infrastructure can reduce horizontal development cost by $2M–$10M per project.

Contact: Austin Planning Department, North Burnet/Gateway team — austintexas.gov/department/austin-planning

2.5 — Industrial / Logistics Land Banking

Round Rock / Pflugerville / Hutto Industrial Corridor — Land Banking

Priority: High Timeline: Immediate

Austin's industrial market absorbed 4.1M SF in 2025 driven by semiconductor supply chain (Samsung Taylor fab, applied materials), logistics, and data center-adjacent users. The strongest BTS (build-to-suit) sub-markets are: Round Rock (established Dell-adjacent corporate campus corridor, I-35 access, $4–$8/SF land), Pflugerville (active data center corridor, incentives available via Chapter 380 through city, $3–$7/SF land), Hutto/Taylor (Samsung semiconductor campus proximity, raw land $2–$5/SF with utility upgrades needed), and Kyle/Buda (I-35 South logistics corridor, growing BTR residential co-location, $5–$9/SF). $842M in industrial investment was tracked in the Austin MSA in 2025. Hines's BTS industrial expertise from other markets applies here.

Land banking scale: 50–500+ acres; $2–$9/SF depending on submarket; total land cost $5M–$50M. BTS developments typically $80–$150/SF all-in.

2.6 — Data Center Land

Pflugerville / Round Rock Data Center Campus Land

Priority: High Timeline: 6-Month

JLL analysis projects Texas will become the national leader in data center capacity by 2030. KDC recently proposed a 150-acre data center campus in Taylor, Texas (adjacent to Samsung's fab). Pflugerville and Round Rock have active Chapter 312/381 abatement programs for large capital investments. Microsoft has a $400M data center in San Antonio. The Pflugerville-Round Rock-Taylor corridor has existing 345kV transmission infrastructure, water access, and municipal incentive programs. Hines could pursue data center land as either (a) a speculative land bank for a hyperscale JV partner, or (b) a direct development partnership with a hyperscale operator (Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta all have Texas land acquisition programs).

Data center land: 50–500+ acres; $2–$10/SF raw in Taylor/Hutto, $8–$20/SF in Pflugerville/Round Rock. Campus developments $500M–$5B total investment.

Bisnow reported Elon Musk selected Austin for a $20B "Terafab Project" (March 2026) — this signals continued utility infrastructure investment in the Austin MSA that increases data center land value in the NE corridor.

Austin-Adjacent Greenfield MPC Parcels — Post-Mirador Assessment

Hines's deal sourcing at this scale is relationship-driven. The most actionable partnership opportunities are the public-sector P3 vehicles (City of Austin, ATP, UT Austin), sovereign wealth / institutional JV equity partners, and the Austin construction supply chain for large mixed-use. 5. Regulatory & Incentive Opportunities — Austin / Texas

Austin has multiple overlapping incentive programs; the key for Hines is identifying which stack of programs applies to each project and pursuing them simultaneously. The HOME 2.0 ordinance, Affordability Unlocked, Chapter 380, TIRZ 17, and Opportunity Zone designations can be combined for the right project structure.

Chapter 380 Economic Development Agreements — Austin

Priority: High Timeline: Immediate Engagement

Chapter 380 of the Texas Local Government Code authorizes cities to grant property tax rebates, sales tax grants, and other financial incentives to qualified businesses and developers. Austin has used 380 agreements for major anchor developments. Recent examples include the city approving "New Incentive Awards for Two Austin Businesses" (as noted on Austin Economic Development's 2024 Annual Report). For Hines's 333 Guadalupe project (potential 1.9M SF, $1B+ total investment), a 380 agreement is not only available but strategically important to negotiate before construction financing is committed. The agreement structure for a development project typically rebates a percentage of new property taxes generated by the project over a 10–15 year period.

Potential 380 benefit for a $1B+ development: $20M–$80M in rebated taxes over 10 years, depending on negotiated percentage and city council approval. No public disclosure of existing Hines 380 agreement for 333 Guadalupe.

How to pursue: Submit Incentive Inquiry through Austin Economic Development online portal. Contact Business Incentives division directly. Austin Economic Development — 535 E 5th St, Austin, TX 78701 | austintexas.gov/economic-development

TIRZ 17 — North Burnet / Gateway (Domain Area)

Priority: High Timeline: Immediate

TIRZ 17 (North Burnet/Gateway) captures property tax increment from development within the North Burnet/Gateway regulating plan area (including the Domain submarket) to fund public infrastructure — streets, utilities, parks, and public facilities — that catalyzes additional private development. Hines's 608K SF Domain office pipeline falls within or adjacent to this TIRZ boundary. TIRZ 17 increment funding can be used to offset public infrastructure costs associated with Hines's development, effectively reducing horizontal development costs. Projects within TIRZ 17 benefit from a public infrastructure subsidy that is typically negotiated through a development agreement with the TIRZ board.

TIRZ 17 infrastructure funding: typically $1M–$15M per project depending on public infrastructure scope. Reduces developer cost of streets, utilities, and public open space.

Contact: Austin Economic Development, TIRZ Administration — austintexas.gov/economic-development | Austin TIRZ board coordinates with the TIRZ Reinvestment Zone Board (appointed by City Council)

TIRZ 18 — Mueller Development Area

Priority: Low (Monitor Only) Timeline: 12–18 Month

TIRZ 18 covers the Mueller redevelopment area (former Robert Mueller Municipal Airport site, ~700 acres, northeast of downtown). Catellus is the master developer and is in late-stage buildout — most of the vertical development parcels are committed or built. Remaining development land is limited and controlled by Catellus. Hines is unlikely to win a Mueller parcel given Catellus's exclusive position, but monitoring the TIRZ 18 geographic boundary for adjacent land assemblage opportunities is worthwhile.

Austin Affordability Unlocked — Density Bonus for Mixed-Use

Priority: Medium Timeline: 6-Month

Austin's Affordability Unlocked (AU) development bonus program allows mixed-use projects to exceed base zoning density limits in exchange for providing affordable housing units. Key features: (1) projects can achieve up to 2x base entitlement density, (2) eligible in most Austin zoning districts, (3) requires a percentage of residential units at 60–80% AMI. For Hines's mixed-use projects that include residential components (e.g., any 333 Guadalupe mixed-use residential phase, or East Austin mixed-use), AU can materially increase developable density without requiring a full rezoning — a significant time and cost savings. AU also unlocks height bonuses in some districts.

No direct program cost; requires affordable housing set-asides (typically 10–15% of units at 60–80% AMI). Density bonus value: 20–50% more GBA than base entitlement — substantial economic value.

Contact: Austin Planning Department, Density Bonus Programs — austintexas.gov/department/austin-planning

Austin HOME Initiative / HOME 2.0 — Density Reform

Priority: Medium Timeline: Immediate (Analysis)

Austin's HOME (Home Options for Middle Endeavors) initiative and subsequent HOME 2.0 ordinance passed in 2024 eliminated single-family-only zoning across Austin, allowing up to 3 units by-right on any residential lot. While this primarily affects residential infill, it has downstream effects on land value in Austin neighborhoods that were previously protected from multifamily development, and affects the competitive land market for sites Hines might consider for mixed-use residential. The ordinance also affects density calculations for sites near the downtown core that have residential components. Hines's Austin land-use counsel should model the HOME 2.0 impact on any sites with residential entitlements in the pipeline.

No direct program cost. Analytical effort: 1–2 weeks of land-use counsel time to model impact on 333 Guadalupe residential components and any East Austin residential mixed-use sites.

Texas Enterprise Zone Program

Priority: Medium Timeline: 6-Month

The Texas Enterprise Zone Program provides state sales and use tax refunds for qualified investments in designated economically distressed areas. Designations are competitive and require city/county nomination. Applications are reviewed by the Texas Economic Development and Tourism Office. For Hines, the Enterprise Zone is most relevant for industrial/BTS projects in eligible suburban corridors (certain tracts in Pflugerville, Hutto, and East Austin may qualify). The refund ranges from $2,500 per job (with 25+ new jobs) to maximum caps depending on capital investment level — up to $5,000 per job for large investments.

Tax refund value: $2,500–$5,000 per qualifying new job created; maximum refund depends on designation tier. For a large BTS industrial project creating 200+ jobs, potential refund of $500K–$1M+.

Contact: Texas Economic Development — governor.texas.gov/economic-development/enterprise-zones | Application requires nominating city/county

Federal Opportunity Zones — IRS December 31, 2026 Deadline

Priority: High Timeline: Immediate — Deferral Deadline Dec 31, 2026

The December 31, 2026 IRS deferral deadline means any previously deferred OZ gains must be recognized by that date, regardless of fund status. For Hines to access OZ economics on an Austin project, a Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF) investment must be made and the project must be in a designated census tract. Austin's OZ tracts are concentrated in East Austin, Rundberg/North Lamar, and portions of Southeast Austin — many of which overlap with the ATP light rail alignment. A QOF structured around a TOD-adjacent East Austin development could capture: (1) transit access value, (2) OZ appreciation tax elimination on 10+ year hold, and (3) potential AHFC affordable housing contribution for density bonus. The OZ/TOD/affordable housing stacked structure is an institutional-scale play that few developers have the complexity capacity to execute — Hines does.

OZ economics: 10+ year hold eliminates appreciation tax entirely. On a $100M development appreciating to $200M over 10+ years, OZ saves $20M+ in federal taxes for investors. Fund structure requires QOF formation (legal costs ~$100K–$300K).

IRS resources: irs.gov/credits-deductions/opportunity-zones-frequently-asked-questions

Travis County / City Tax Abatements (Chapter 312 / 381)

Priority: Medium Timeline: 6-Month

Texas Chapter 312 authorizes cities and counties to offer property tax abatements for qualifying commercial and industrial projects. Travis County and the City of Austin both have abatement programs for large capital investment projects. The most active users are data centers, semiconductor fabs, and large manufacturing (Samsung Taylor received multi-billion abatement packages). For Hines's data center land play in the NE corridor (Taylor, Pflugerville), a Chapter 312 abatement from Williamson County or the relevant municipality can significantly improve the economic case for land banking and development. Abatements are typically 50–100% of incremental property tax for 5–10 years.

Chapter 312 abatement: 50–100% of incremental property taxes for 5–10 years. On a $200M data center facility with $2M/year in property taxes, a 10-year abatement = $10M–$20M in savings.

7.2 — 333 Guadalupe Competitive Context: Downtown Austin Tower Pipeline

Downtown Austin Office/Mixed-Use Tower Pipeline (2025–2026):

Austin's downtown development pipeline is constrained by the 17–19% office vacancy rate, which is suppressing speculative new office starts. The competitive set for 333 Guadalupe's potential 1.9M SF at Guadalupe/11th Street (GLO ground lease) includes:

  • Brandywine 405 Colorado — Downtown office development in Brandywine's pipeline; visionary development stage.
  • Uptown ATX (Brandywine) — 66-acre North Austin mixed-use at Burnet Rd (competing for Class A office tenants with Domain product, not directly with downtown 333 Guadalupe)
  • School-to-housing adaptive reuse — Broke ground March 2026; adaptive reuse projects are absorbing some downtown infill capacity but not competing for same institutional tenants.
  • Haven Housing suburban apartment acquisition — Multifamily, not a direct 333 Guadalupe competitor.

The primary near-term risk for 333 Guadalupe is the absence of an anchor tenant commitment. In a 17–19% vacancy environment, construction lenders and institutional equity partners will require a significant pre-leasing threshold (typically 30–50% of office GBA) before funding construction. Hines's strategy should prioritize anchor tenant prospecting and LOI execution over land acquisition in the next 6 months.

7.3 — Office-to-Residential Conversion Opportunity

Austin Distressed Office-to-Residential / Office-to-Lab Conversion

Priority: Medium Timeline: 12–18 Month Assessment

Austin's 17–19% office vacancy creates conversion opportunity for older, functionally obsolete Class B/C office assets — particularly those built pre-2000 with floor plates below 25,000 SF (better suited for residential conversion). Austin's rental prices dropped 7% year-over-year (per Austin Current coverage) but remain high, and the HOME 2.0 upzoning has increased residential land value. Hines could evaluate distressed office assets in the $50–$150/SF range for conversion to multifamily or life sciences lab. Targets should be concentrated in East Austin and near the Dell Medical District / UT campus where conversion economics work best. Ten-X Commercial is one monitoring channel for distressed asset auctions.

Conversion projects: acquisition $50–$150/SF for Class B/C office; conversion cost $100–$200/SF for residential; total all-in $150–$350/SF. Viable where residential stabilized value exceeds $300/SF.

7.4 — Build-to-Rent (BTR) Land Market

Austin BTR Active Submarkets — Cedar Park, Kyle, Buda, Hutto

Priority: Medium Timeline: 12–18 Month

Austin's BTR (build-to-rent single-family) market is active in the suburban ring following Mirador's divestiture. Active BTR sub-markets: Kyle/Buda (I-35 South; land $5–$10/SF; proximity to Tesla Gigafactory workforce), Cedar Park (NW suburb; land $8–$15/SF; established master plan infrastructure), Hutto/Taylor (NE; Samsung/NXP workforce demand; land $3–$7/SF). BTR operators seeking JV land partners with institutional capital: NexMetro Communities, Wan Bridge, LGI Homes, and Bungalo (Invitation Homes subsidiary). Hines's post-Mirador posture on residential land should clarify whether BTR is in-scope before pursuing BTR land partnerships.

BTR community: 100–300 homes; land 20–60 acres; total investment $30M–$120M. JV structures typically 90/10 or 80/20 equity splits with BTR operator contributing operations, institutional capital contributing land + construction equity.

7.5 — Life Sciences / Lab Real Estate

Austin Life Sciences — Dell Medical / UT Health Austin Demand Drivers

Priority: Medium Timeline: 12–18 Month

Austin's life sciences real estate market is early-stage but growing. Key demand drivers: Dell Medical School (UT Austin campus, opened 2016, generating growing clinical research demand), UT Health Austin (clinical practice partner), Texas Bioscience Institute, and a nascent biotech startup ecosystem. Austin lacks purpose-built lab/GMP space — a gap that institutional developers in Boston and San Diego have exploited. Hines's mixed-use development capability could extend to a life sciences district adjacent to Dell Medical if UT Austin offers a ground lease vehicle. Current Austin life sciences demand is estimated at 500K–1M SF; the market is not yet at institutional critical mass for speculative development, but a pre-leased BTS lab building on UT or Dell Medical-adjacent land (if a UT ground lease or AHFC land contribution is available) is feasible.

Life sciences lab: construction cost $400–$700/SF (significantly higher than conventional office). Viable only with strong anchor pre-lease or UT/hospital system co-commitment.

7.6 — Data Center Development in Depth

Round Rock / Pflugerville / Taylor Data Center Corridor:

JLL analysis (2025) projects Texas to become the US national leader in data center capacity by 2030. Key data points for Hines's decision framework:

  • KDC: proposed 150-acre data center campus in Taylor, TX (adjacent to Samsung fab)
  • Microsoft: $400M data center in San Antonio (signals appetite for Texas hyperscale investment)
  • Elon Musk's $20B "Terafab" project selected Austin (March 2026) — likely to require significant power and land infrastructure investment in the MSA
  • Pflugerville and Round Rock both have active Chapter 312/381 abatement programs for large capital investment projects — the specific contact is each city's EDC
  • Land values: Round Rock $8–$20/SF (developed with infrastructure); Taylor/Hutto raw $2–$5/SF (requires utility upgrades, most large data centers negotiate utility commitments directly with LCRA or city utilities)

Hines's value-add in this segment: the ability to structure a 50–150 acre data center campus with institutional-grade site preparation, permitting relationships, and JV capitalization — reducing time to power for hyperscale operators who pay a premium for shovel-ready sites.
