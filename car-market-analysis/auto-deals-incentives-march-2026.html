Executive Summary

Key Findings: The expiration of federal EV purchase credits (September 2025) has fundamentally shifted incentive strategy—manufacturers are now relying on dealer-funded rebates (up to $10,000) and zero-percent financing (49 offers nationally). Hybrid vehicles are the growth leader (+9.1% YoY in February 2026), while EV market softens. Average new vehicle prices remain elevated at ~$49,170, with tariff pressures anticipated to add 3% inflation in 2026. Austin mirrors national trends with 59% SUV/crossover dominance, but local EV share is projected to decline to ~6% by year-end. For buyers, the new IRA §25E loan interest deduction (up to $10,000/year) favors purchases over leases for electric vehicles.

Sub-Lease & Lease Buyout Market

Lease Transfer (Sub-Lease): Platforms like Swapalease.com and LeaseTrader.com enable mid-term lease transfers. This strategy is useful when acquiring a short remaining lease term (6–18 months) with rates locked in before rate changes.

Lease Buyout Landscape:

  • Average buyout loan APR: 9.01% (all credit profiles)
  • Excellent credit buyout APR: 6.18%
  • Average amount financed: $31,577
  • Average monthly payment: $570.53

Strategic rationale: In a market with ~$49,170 average new vehicle prices, buying out a lease with a below-market residual can outpace purchasing a new car.

Powertrain Trends

Hybrid Surge (+9.1% YoY, February 2026):

  • 161,580 hybrid units sold in February 2026
  • Toyota dominates with 41.6% of all HEV sales
  • 33% of shoppers targeting hybrids for next purchase
  • Hybrids increasingly preferred over EVs post-tax-credit expiration

EV Market Softening:

  • EV share of US sales: ~9.1% (2025)
  • EV share projected 2026: ~11.8% nationally, ~6% in Texas
  • Decline attributed to federal credit expiration and improved hybrid economics

2026 Outlook

Sales Forecast: US new vehicle SAAR projected at 15.8–16.0 million units (Cox Automotive, Edmunds), representing a slight decline from 2025.

Tariff Impact: J.P. Morgan estimates ~3% inflation from tariffs by mid-2026; automakers currently absorbing costs, but price shock expected in Q2–Q3 as absorption capacity exhausted.

Key Headwinds: Affordability concerns, high interest rates, tariff uncertainty, and federal credit expiration.
