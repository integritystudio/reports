Founding a 501(c)(3) Movement Arts Nonprofit in Austin

Legal formation, funding, safety & the evidence base for a community movement-arts organization

Research & implementation guide · Austin, Texas · Prepared July 2026

Recommended path

A movement-arts nonprofit supporting existing Austin artists, events, and venues — flow arts, firespinning, acroyoga, contact improv, ecstatic dance, partner dance, aerial acrobatics, performance choreography, and weight-lifting — with an explicit commitment to safety and empowerment for LGBTQ+ individuals, grounded in research on the mental-health benefits of dance and community. Named community partners: Flowstorm, Swolemates, and Ekstasis at Brodie Homestead.

  1. Launch under a fiscal sponsor first, incorporate later. Operate immediately under an Austin fiscal sponsor (Austin Creative Alliance or Museum of Human Achievement) to accept tax-deductible donations and apply for grants now — no waiting on the IRS.
  2. Incorporate as a Texas nonprofit corporation (Certificate of Formation, Form 202, $25) with a purpose clause and asset-dedication language drafted to satisfy the IRS — not the bare state template.
  3. Get an EIN, adopt IRS-compliant bylaws + conflict-of-interest policy, seat a board of ≥3 directors, then file federal exemption on Form 1023-EZ ($275) if eligible, or Form 1023 ($600).
  4. After the IRS determination letter, file Texas franchise & sales-tax exemption, then register with the Texas Commission on the Arts and City of Austin cultural-arts programs.
  5. Stand up a real safety & insurance program for fire/flow/aerial before the first event (NAFAA standards + specialty coverage).
  6. Anchor your charitable-purpose narrative in peer-reviewed evidence that social dance and community belonging improve mental health, with LGBTQ+-specific literature backing your safety commitment.

1. Legal & procedural formation

Verified · high confidence IRS primary sources, 3-vote unanimous.

1a. Form the Texas nonprofit corporation

  • File a Certificate of Formation – Nonprofit Corporation (Form 202) with the Texas Secretary of State; filing fee $25. [nonprofitquest.com; 501c3.org]
  • Form 202 requires: name, registered agent, initial board, purpose clause, member structure, and organizer info. [nonprofitquest.com]
  • A Texas nonprofit must have at least three directors. [nonprofitquest.com]
  • Your purpose must include a recognized exempt characteristic — charitable and educational fit best here (arts education, community health, public performance). [nonprofitquest.com]
Critical gotcha: The state's Form 202 template does not by itself meet IRS requirements for 501(c)(3) status. Your Certificate of Formation must add an IRS-compliant purpose clause (limiting activities to exempt purposes) and a dissolution / asset-dedication clause permanently dedicating assets to a 501(c)(3) purpose. [501c3.org – TX guide; IRS i1023ez]

confirm Standard companion steps: appoint a registered agent with a Texas street address; adopt bylaws and a conflict-of-interest policy; hold an organizational board meeting; obtain a free EIN.

1b. Obtain an EIN

An organization must obtain an EIN before applying for 501(c)(3) status (free, via IRS). [donorbox.org]

1c. Apply for federal 501(c)(3) recognition

 Form 1023-EZ (streamlined)Form 1023 (standard)
User fee$275$600
FilingElectronic only, via Pay.gov (no paper)Electronic, via Pay.gov
EligibilityTotal assets < $250,000 and gross revenue < $50,000; must pass Eligibility WorksheetNo size cap; required if you fail the EZ worksheet

Sources: donorbox.org; IRS – About Form 1023; IRS – Instructions for Form 1023-EZ

  • Form 1023-EZ requires the mandatory Eligibility Worksheet first. Answering "Yes" to any question disqualifies you and forces the full Form 1023. [IRS i1023ez]
  • EZ eligibility is gated by the gross-receipts and total-asset limits you attest to on the worksheet. [IRS i1023ez]
  • Your organizing document must permanently dedicate assets to a section 501(c)(3) purpose. [IRS i1023ez]
  • Both forms are filed electronically through Pay.gov. [IRS – About Form 1023]
Planning note: The activities (dance, fitness, performance, arts education) are ordinary exempt purposes. The IRS will focus on your purpose clause, public-benefit orientation, and financials. Because you intend to support existing groups (Flowstorm, Swolemates, Ekstasis), draft the purpose around public arts education, community wellness, and charitable support of the local movement-arts field — not around any single private business.

1d. Texas state tax exemptions confirm

After the IRS determination letter, apply to the Texas Comptroller (typically Form AP-204) for exemption from Texas franchise and sales/use tax — a separate step from federal recognition. Not surfaced in fetched sources; verify current process at comptroller.texas.gov.

2. Governance & fiscal sponsorship

Verified · high confidence Public Counsel + CreateAustin + sponsor primary pages, 3-vote unanimous.

Recommendation: begin under a fiscal sponsor. Fiscal sponsorship lets an unincorporated individual or group carry out charitable programs and accept tax-deductible donations and grants through an existing 501(c)(3) public charity (the "Sponsor") — without incorporating, applying for exemption, or maintaining nonprofit status yourself. Sponsors typically charge an administrative fee of 5–15% of funds collected, scaled to services provided. Austin has unusually strong options:

  • Austin Creative Alliance (ACA) — the single most actionable launch resource. ACA extends its 501(c)(3) over community-based Austin arts projects, so you can receive tax-deductible donations and grants immediately without incorporating first. Cost: $180 annual membership + 10% revenue share on donations/grants. ACA prioritizes projects contributing to Austin's cultural vibrancy — a direct fit for supporting Flowstorm, Swolemates, and Ekstasis. [austincreativealliance.org]
  • Museum of Human Achievement (MoHA) — sponsors individual artists and informal/LLC organizations, prioritizes Central Texas projects, and charges no fiscal-sponsorship fee. Strong mission alignment: its grantee portfolio is ~61% LGBTQIA+ (it distributed ~$425K to 140 first-time grantees). Unusually favorable for a grassroots, LGBTQ+-affirming startup. [moha.wiki]
  • Create Austin — "A Guide to Using Fiscal Sponsors" — an Austin-specific guide for comparing sponsor terms (fee vs. no-fee, control, services). [createaustin.org]
City-grant tip: for City of Austin HOT-funded grants, the City caps the fiscal-sponsor service fee at 10% of the total grant award — so a sponsored project keeps at least 90% of any City grant. [createaustin.org]

How to choose: compare fee structure (ACA's $180 + 10% vs. MoHA's no-fee), back-office services (bookkeeping, insurance, grant admin), how much control the sponsor retains, and whether they'll cover the liability profile of fire/aerial events. Many groups run 1–2 years under sponsorship, build a track record and donor base, then graduate to independent 501(c)(3) status.

Governance foundations regardless of path: a board of ≥3 unrelated directors, bylaws, a conflict-of-interest policy, and clear financial controls — all expected by the IRS and grantmakers.

3. Austin & Texas arts funding

Verified · high confidence City of Austin & TCA state-agency primary sources, 3-vote unanimous. Dollar amounts time-sensitive — confirm current cycle.

3a. City of Austin — cultural arts grants (ACME)

The Austin Arts, Culture, Music and Entertainment (ACME) office administers the main local cultural-arts grants, funded by Hotel Occupancy Tax (HOT). [austintexas.gov]

Program tiers — where a new org fits:

  • Nexus (entry tier) — $5,000 and $10,000 awards over 12-month periods for emerging/established artists and arts groups. Requires ≥1 year Austin-MSA history and budget ≤$150K. The realistic entry point. [austintexas.gov]
  • Elevate (mid tier) — accepts 501(c)(3) nonprofits, arts groups with an EIN, or individual artists — the most flexible eligibility, workable via a fiscal sponsor. [austintexas.gov]
  • Thrive (top tier) — multi-year operational funding (~$85K–$150K/yr) restricted to 501(c)(3) arts nonprofits with ≥5 years Austin/MSA history, over half of production in Austin/ETJ, and annual expenses ≥$60,000 per Form 990. A brand-new org will not yet qualify — build toward it. [austintexas.gov/thrive]

Apply / ask: City portal + culturalarts@austintexas.gov.

time-sensitive Context: in FY26 the City awarded 731 grants ($24M+); 3.3% of awardees were in Dance and 26.6% identified as LGBTQ — funding demonstrably reaches dance and LGBTQ+ arts communities. [austintexas.gov]

3b. Texas Commission on the Arts (TCA)

  • TCA grants are restricted to Texas-based 501(c)(3) nonprofits and governmental entities — individuals and fiscally sponsored/umbrella proposals are ineligible. [arts.texas.gov]
  • Organizations with an arts mission and a verified budget of $4,000+ can apply for Arts Respond Project grants (avg $5,000; deadlines July 15 and January 15). [arts.texas.gov]
  • First-time nonprofit applicants must email their IRS letter of determination to accounts@arts.texas.gov to set up a grant-portal account (up to 5 business days); competitive grants require a SAM-UEI number. [arts.texas.gov]
Implication: To access TCA money you generally need your own 501(c)(3); a fiscal sponsor does not make you TCA-eligible (unlike City of Austin programs). A key reason to pursue independent incorporation once established.

3c. Funding roadmap

  1. Now (fiscal-sponsored): City of Austin Nexus (via sponsor), private/community foundation grants, crowdfunding.
  2. After own 501(c)(3): register with TCA; obtain SAM-UEI; pursue TCA grants.
  3. After 5 yrs + $60k budget: City of Austin Thrive multi-year funding.

4. Insurance, liability & safety

Directional — not independently verified This section draws on industry search results (NAFAA, specialty insurers, Flow Arts Institute) that fell outside the verified claim set — the verification pass confirmed no insurance/liability findings. Treat as a starting checklist and confirm specifics with a licensed insurance broker and the Austin Fire Department.

Fire, flow, and aerial arts carry real physical risk; insurers and the Austin Fire Department (AFD) will require documented safety practices. Build this before your first event.

Adopt recognized fire-arts safety standards

  • NAFAA (North American Fire Arts Association) Performer Safety Guidelines — the recognized industry-standard body. Covers tool maintenance, wick attachment, spotters/safeties, extinguisher ratings, and audience control — the reference venues and fire marshals expect in a fire-arts org's SOPs. [NAFAA / avon.org]
  • Specialty Insurance Agency — Safety Requirements for Fire Performers — an insurer's own conditions of coverage (dedicated fire safety/spotter, ABC-rated extinguishers, controlled wick/fuel handling, crash-zone clearances). Usable directly as an event checklist. [specialtyinsuranceagency.com]

Fire-marshal / open-flame permits

Flow Arts Institute — "How to Approach Your Local Fire Marshal to Legally Spin Fire" — practical guide to open-flame performance permission; AFD requires open-flame performance permits for public fire events. [flowartsinstitute.com]

Insurance to secure

General liability + event/special-event liability, participant accident coverage, and directors & officers (D&O) coverage for the board. Aerial and fire acts typically require a specialty/excess carrier; standard nonprofit GL often excludes them — get written confirmation that fire and aerial are covered, not excluded. [specialty carriers: XInsurance, Insureon, Specialty Insurance Agency]

Operational safety program

Written SOPs per discipline (fire safety officer & extinguishers; rigging inspection, qualified riggers, crash mats, load ratings for aerial; spotting protocols for acro), waivers/assumption-of-risk forms, incident reporting, and instructor qualification standards.

5. Evidence: dance, community & mental health

Verified · high confidence Peer-reviewed 2024 primary sources, 3-vote confirmed. (One meta-analysis's RCT label and small sample warrant modest caution.)

This literature is the backbone of your charitable-purpose narrative and grant "case for support." Two findings were independently confirmed: social/community dance is a documented accessible, low-cost approach to preventing and treating mood disorders, and LGBTQ+ community events measurably promote acceptance, belonging, empowerment, and mental-health resilience.

  • Social dance and movement for mental health: A narrative review (Delattre et al., 2024, Mental Health Science) — peer-reviewed synthesis of psychology, neuroscience, and psychiatry evidence that social forms of dance (not just clinical dance/movement therapy) benefit mental health and wellbeing. Maps directly to partner dance, contact improv, ecstatic dance, and community events. [Wiley, doi 10.1002/mhs2.62]
  • Effects of Dance Movement Therapy and Dance on Health-Related Psychological Outcomes — systematic review/meta-analysis quantifying dance's psychological benefits. [NCBI PMC6710484]
  • LGBTQ+ community, belonging & mental health — "Community, belonging and acceptance; is this the antidote to shame and societal discrimination?" (Psychology & Sexuality, 2024). Empirically links being in a safe space with others to reduced sexuality-related shame, framing community/belonging/acceptance as protective against discrimination-driven distress. Directly supports the org's LGBTQ+ safety commitment. [Taylor & Francis, doi 10.1080/19419899.2024.2372047]
How to use this: In your purpose clause and grant narratives, position the charitable purpose as advancing community mental health and social connection through movement arts, with safe, affirming spaces for LGBTQ+ participants as a core public-benefit outcome — each claim anchored to the citations above. This strengthens both IRS "charitable/educational purpose" framing and competitive grant scoring.

6. Comparable & model organizations

Proven templates for mission language, governance, and the "support existing events" model:

  • Full Moon Jam Foundation (Austin) — a local fire/flow arts nonprofit; the closest hometown model for community fire-arts events and their permitting/safety posture. [fullmoonjam.org]
  • Seattle Flow Arts Collective (SFAC) — a 501(c)(3) founded to expand programming and policy advocacy for flow arts and fire spinning regionally. Mission ("promote well-being and inspire play by sharing and advancing the flow arts") is a proven template for a regional collective supporting local artists rather than running one venue. [mightycause.com/seattleflowarts]
  • Spinning Arts Foundation / WildFire Retreat — a 501(c)(3) (est. 2013) that "functions as a resource for those wishing to start their own events, programs, or organizations." A direct governance model for an umbrella foundation backing multiple community-run fire/flow events and handling event insurance and safety — mirroring your intent to support Flowstorm, Ekstasis, etc. [wildfireretreat.org]
  • Circus / aerial-arts nonprofits (e.g. CSAW) — model for aerial rigging, instruction, and safety governance. [csawcircus.org — review directly]
Pattern to emulate: a foundation/umbrella that supports the existing ecosystem (events, artists, venues) rather than competing with it — pooling insurance, safety standards, fiscal infrastructure, and grant access on behalf of partners like Flowstorm, Swolemates, and Ekstasis.
